Droplets of Dew

A Breathtaking Psychological Film In Your Hands

In this novel, you stand before a man for whom prison walls were not his true confinement. Instead, his mask was the cage—fused so deeply to his skin that tearing it away meant bleeding shards of his own soul. Reading "Yousef" is not just following events; it is watching "Droplets of Dew" fall slowly upon a shattered consciousness, washing away the dust of twenty-five years of silence and injustice.

The novel leaves you with a bitter question: Are we truly who we see in the mirror? Or are we merely "versions" crafted by expectations and disappointments? It is a journey into the philosophy of pain; the hero does not seek innocence proven by papers, but seeks his "lost face" amidst a crowd of masks.

Despite the darkness of the beginnings, the author leaves you with a thin thread of light—like that which seeps through the cracks of an old cell—to tell you that a scar is not a mark of ugliness, but the only proof that you survived. It is a call to stop apologizing to the world for our truth and to start searching for the "home" that lives within us.

Chapter I: When the Face Fell

"Some scream to be heard... others bleed in silence, praying that the scars will speak. This is not a cry for help, but a mirror held in the dark, waiting for a soul brave enough to ignite a flame."

My name is Yousef. I did not survive; I disappeared... and this is all that remains. It is said that a person does not truly know themselves until they love, but I learned that love does not know you—it strips you until nothing is left but a tiny core hiding in a forgotten corner of your soul.

I walked through the city like someone dragging their shadow on the asphalt, unsure whether I was looking for a path or running from one. Have you ever felt that your existence walks one step ahead of you? That you are following yourself as if tracing an echo you haven't made yet?

That feeling, my friend, is what some call "consciousness," and what I call the "curse" that made me see more than I should. Everything here repeats itself... the same streets, the same faces, and the same fear disguised as a smile.
